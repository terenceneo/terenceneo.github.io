Semester 4 Module Review

Review of modules taken this sem that a fellow EE student might consider taking.
Reviews are opinionated and will be biased. Please avoid reading if you will be offended.

EE2033 Integrated System Lab

EE2033 Overview

Note that although NUSMods currently lists EE2033 as a Sem 2 module, it should also be conducted in Sem 1s as well.

The whole point of this mod is to build a filter using opamps. A message signal with noise will be transmitted, you will be on the recieving end. The task is to build a filter to remove the noise from the received signal in order to increase the percentage of the message successfully recovered. Throughout the sem, there will be lectures and labs that progressively builds up your knowledge so that you will eventually be equipped enough to build this filter. Success in the project is rather random, independent of the theory taught, comms is such a fickle thing. It is however, dependent on how much effort you put in to trial and error more.

Unlike previous iterations aka EE2031/32, all materials required for the construction will be provided with almost no need for you to purchase additional stuff from Sim Lim Square.

Labs will require knowledge of PC2020, EE2023 and EE2027. Contrary to how this sounds, a general , superficial impression of what you have learnt in these 3 mods should suffice as the knowledge required is mostly self contained with a brief theory presented at the start of each pre-lab. Safe to take in Y4. Of course, it’s going to save you some time if you have good foundations, but not knowing your stuff will not really impact your grades.

EE2033 Workload

  • 4x 2hr lectures in the first 4 weeks. Not attending these lectures will have minimum impact on your learning and grades for this module.
  • 6x 3hr weekly labs (usually 9am-12pm slots). This inlucdes a graded weekly pre-lab assignment that is done as homework, and a lab report to be completed during the lab session. If you remain on task and collaborate, you should be able to complete the lab reports by the 1.5-2hr mark giving you ample time to have lunch and travel to your next lesson at 12pm. The pre-labs are relatively straight forward and can be completed in <1hr + approx 30mins verifying your answers with friends.
  • Mini-project that spans the last 4 weeks of the module, pair work. This mini-project is really the focus of the module, and I would say perhaps the differentiating factor that determines your grade. This project is probably what makes the module worth it’s 4 MCs (think doing 1MC worth of work per week?), so avoid other modules with a crunch time around weeks 12-13ish. However, as you only have 1 toolkit to share among 2 people in the group, you will probably be working on the project for an average of less than 2 weeks aka only 2 weeks of real work required for the whole sem for this mod. That said, this is the component that most people found rather challenging probably because they spent lots of time trying to reason experimental data with theory.

EE2033 Recommendations

Given the low workload of this module (much unlike that of other EE2k mods), the recommendation would be to use this as a balance for a heavy semester’s workload. Perhaps the strategy can be similiar to how one might utilise a GE mod. The ideal scenario would be to gather a group of friends (your grades for this mod are after all heavily reliant on your ability to collaborate) and take this module together in Y2S2, together with the poly batch. However, in the event this is not possible due to pre-req reasons, taking this module in NUS would be ideal with the mentality that this module will free up time to do better in your other modules.

EE2033 is unlikely to be a pre-req for most TEs and hence taking it in Y4 (after SEP/IA in Y3) should not be an issue.

EE2033 Tips to Excel

  • It is of outmost importance to get full or almost full marks for all lab reports (Averages at 98-99 / 100). This is the only component of your grade for this module that you have control over.
  • On the first day of the project, draw around 2-3 of each opamp. The opamps they have in stock was not enough to cater to the demands of the module (and you know, some people hoard opamps). It would be non-ideal to waste time begging around for materials at the crunch and end up with slipshod work. Expect to use 2-3 opamp chips for your final design.
  • Don’t bother about Windows Subsystem for Linux, you need a GUI for GNU Radio. You may perhaps consider side-loading Linux onto VirtualBox if you have >=16GB of RAM. Rationale is that you can work with both Windows BitScope and Linus GNU Radio on the same laptop, ability to transfer screenshots to you report in Word easily and avoid wasting time fiddling around with Linux. Take this with a pinch of salt as there are bugs in the programme, still recommended to have a full linux installation to fallback on if you are getting errors on VirtualBox.
  • Get a USB hub if you have not already for EE2028. Expect to need 6 ports (bitscope, powersupply, transmitter, receiver, mouse, thumbdrive) to have an unhindered workflow.
  • For the project, don’t bother too much about the theory, just start building and adjust accordingly, you will realise that experimental results deviate significantly from theory. The strategy to doing this would probably be as they call it in machine learning - a grid search algorithm. This universal magic algo follows:



while (not results == optimal || not deadline) {
    smartly whack something not tried yet
}
